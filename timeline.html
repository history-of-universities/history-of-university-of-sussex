Chronology

From the Charter to the present

A working chronology of the University of Sussex and the long campaign that preceded it. Entries are drawn from the founding record.

  • 1911
    The first proposal.

    A university college for Brighton is proposed at a mayoral banquet at the Royal Pavilion, and pressed at a public meeting that December.

  • 1913
    Royal Commission.

    A Royal Commission recommends extending the Schools of the University of London to the County of Sussex. The First World War halts the appeal; the plan narrowly fails in 1914.

  • 1954
    Policy turns.

    A shift in government policy in December reopens the prospect of new institutions.

  • 1956
    The Stanmer Park site.

    Brighton Council agrees to make available 145 acres at Stanmer Park on a 999-year lease at a peppercorn rent of £1 a year.

  • 1958
    Funding announced.

    The Chancellor of the Exchequer announces a programme of new university building, with funds allocated to the University College of Sussex.

  • 1959
    Incorporated; Fulton appointed.

    The University College of Sussex is registered as a company (20 May). John Fulton is appointed Principal that September.

  • 1961
    Royal Charter; the university opens.

    The Royal Charter of Incorporation is granted on 16 August. Sussex opens in October to 52 students in the arts, in two houses on Preston Road, Brighton. Fulton becomes first Vice-Chancellor; the motto, Be still and know, is chosen.

  • 1962
    Coat of arms granted.

    The College of Arms grants the university its arms (15 March); Sussex is the first British university granted supporters. See The Arms.

  • 1960s
    Falmer and the schools.

    Basil Spence’s campus rises on the Downs; Falmer House is later listed Grade I. The arts and sciences are organised into interdisciplinary schools rather than departments.

  • 1964
    A growing campus.

    The School of African and Asian Studies is established (October). Queen Elizabeth II opens the library on 13 November.

  • 1965–66
    Research institutes.

    The Institute of Development Studies is located at Sussex (1965); the Science Policy Research Unit (SPRU) is founded (1966).

  • 1966–67
    Briggs succeeds Fulton.

    Asa Briggs is elected second Vice-Chancellor (May 1966) and takes up the post in 1967, as Fulton stands down and the ‘Robbins parenthesis’ begins to close.

  • 1968
    Protest, and a name.

    Student protest over the Vietnam War. Michael Beloff’s The Plateglass Universities gives the new foundations their lasting name.

  • 1986–2001
    Research standing.

    Through the research assessment exercises of the period, Sussex ranks among the top fifteen universities in the UK for research.

Drawn from Berry (2024) and the founding record; see Sources.
