The coat of arms · 1962

The arms, and the colours of this site

The University of Sussex received its Royal Charter in 1961. A little over a year later, on 15 March 1962, the College of Arms granted the new university its coat of arms. The grant was designed by Sir Anthony Wagner, then Richmond Herald. Its tinctures are the founding colours from which this site takes its branding.

The blazon

Argent, on a chevron per pale azure and gules six martlets or, between in chief two Saxon crowns and in base a dolphin naiant sable. Grant of arms, College of Arms, 15 March 1962

What the colours mean

Five tinctures

The arms gather the heraldry of the place the university was planted. The chevron carries the colours of the two historic counties; the martlets are the Sussex birds; the crowns recall the Saxon kingdom; the dolphin belongs to Brighton.

Azure

Blue

The dexter half of the chevron. Drawn from the arms of the Sussex counties. The primary identity colour, and the primary colour of this site.

Gules

Red

The sinister half of the chevron, also from the county arms. The accent colour throughout the site.

Or

Gold

The six martlets — the heraldic birds of Sussex — ranged along the chevron. Used here for highlight and detail.

Sable

Black

The two Saxon crowns, for the Kingdom of the South Saxons, and the dolphin, taken from the arms of Brighton.

Argent

Silver / White

The field of the shield. The warm off-white page colour of this site.

A first

Sussex was the first British university to be granted heraldic supporters, the figures that stand either side of a shield — a mark of the standing the new foundation was accorded at its creation.
