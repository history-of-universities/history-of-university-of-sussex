The campus · Falmer

Spence's university on the Downs

The University of Sussex was built at Falmer, in a fold of the South Downs four miles outside Brighton, on 145 acres made available by Brighton Council in 1956 on a 999-year lease at a peppercorn rent of a pound a year. To design it, the university appointed Sir Basil Spence, fresh from his commission for Coventry Cathedral. Sussex believed that a university could give an education of taste through its architecture and landscaping, and Spence was the architect thought able to translate that ambition into a university estate.

The plan

A campus laid out on the Downs

Spence planned the campus as a single composition rather than a collection of separate buildings, working with the contours of the valley and the chalk landscape around it. An Ove Arup site model set out the relation of the buildings to one another and to the Downs, and successive plans worked through the placing of the library, the lecture theatres, and the residential and meeting spaces.

The architecture had a deliberate visual vocabulary: red brick and board-marked concrete, and a rhythm of round arches that gave the early buildings a unity recognisable across the site. It was an expensive vision. Spence’s disregard for budgets and his cost over-runs earned him the affectionate nickname ‘Sir Basil-Expense’.

Not every element was realised. A Vice-Chancellor’s house designed for the campus was never built, and individual schemes were revised as funds allowed. But the essential character, an integrated modern campus in brick and concrete, was carried through from plan to ground.

Plans and drawings

Falmer House

The heart of the campus

At the centre of the campus stood Falmer House, the building that gathered the social and ceremonial life of the university. Built around a courtyard and a moat, in brick and concrete with Spence’s characteristic round-arched openings, it became the emblem of Sussex and one of the most admired university buildings of its generation. It is now listed Grade I, the only postwar university building in the country to hold that distinction at the time of its listing.

The placing of sculpture on the campus, including a Henry Moore, was of a piece with the founding belief that the pictures on the walls and the architecture of a university were part of its teaching. The moat and courtyard of Falmer House gave the building a sense of enclosure and ceremony appropriate to the centre of a new university.

Building the university

A campus under construction

Through the early and mid-1960s the campus rose in stages: the arts buildings, the library, the meeting house, the science laboratories, and the residential houses. The university opened in 1961 in temporary accommodation in Brighton while Falmer was built, and moved onto the new site as the buildings came into use.

By 1970 the essential campus was in place, an integrated modern environment in a downland setting, recognisably the work of one architectural imagination. It is this campus, as much as its curriculum, that fixed the image of Sussex as the first of the plate-glass universities.

Buildings and grounds

Falmer House was the centre, but Spence’s campus was a whole environment: the Meeting House, the residential College House, the arts and science buildings, and the landscaped grounds with their sculpture and ponds.
