The motto · 1961

Be still and know

Of all the decisions John Fulton took as Sussex’s first Vice-Chancellor, the choice of motto was among the most revealing. Be still and know, settled on in 1961, looks at first like a gentle invitation to reflection. Read more closely, against Fulton’s philosophical formation, it is something more exacting: an instruction to pause, to withhold precipitate judgement, and to examine before assenting. The motto names the discipline the whole university was built to cultivate.

A verse cut in half

The deliberate truncation

The words come from Psalm 46, verse 10, whose full text reads ‘Be still, and know that I am God’. Fulton kept the opening and cut the rest. The amputation matters. Removing ‘that I am God’ preserves the structure of contemplative withdrawal while quietly secularising its content: the verse no longer commands stillness in order to recognise divine authority, but stillness in order to know, without saying what is to be known.

Fulton was candid that the choice was his own devising, that he wanted it in English rather than Latin, and that it should come from the Bible or Shakespeare. He recalled the phrase coming to mind while walking in the Scottish hills. Yet when later asked directly why he had omitted ‘that I am God’, he refused to answer. The refusal was itself a kind of teaching. To explain the omission would be to close the question; leaving it open turned the motto into a standing invitation to ask what knowing means, and what the pause is for.

The pause

An ancient discipline: aproptosia

Behind Fulton’s motto lies a very old idea. The Stoics distinguished aproptosia, the virtue of non-precipitation, the habit of examining each impression carefully before assenting to it, from propeteia, the vice of rushing to judgement. Most human error, they held, comes not from faulty reasoning but from premature assent to representations that have not been properly tested. As Epictetus put it, ‘it is not things themselves that disturb people, but their judgements about those things’.

The remedy was a discipline, practised daily: examine the impression, test whether it corresponds to reality, and withhold assent until clarity is reached. The aim was never paralysis but better judgement, learning to pause long enough to examine carefully, but not so long as to become incapable of acting. To ‘be still’, in this register, is to refuse the precipitate verdict and to hold judgement in suspense while the work of examination is done.

Descartes

The discipline of methodical doubt

Fulton read this ancient discipline through Descartes, in whom Pierre Hadot saw not a dry epistemologist but the inheritor of philosophy as spiritual exercise. The Meditations begin by withdrawing from ordinary life into a quiet space and systematically doubting every previous belief, granting assent to nothing until what is ‘clear and distinct’ emerges. This is the Stoic suspension of judgement turned into method: an institutionalised pause that clears the ground before knowledge is rebuilt on secure foundations.

Trained in this Cartesian tradition at Balliol, Fulton understood education in precisely these terms. In his 1962 essay ‘The Shape of Universities’ he argued that education must instil ‘a methodical sceptical approach that questions every step in our thinking, a refusal to take things for granted’. The motto names that approach. Be still is the withdrawal, the deliberate hesitation; and know is the knowledge that only careful examination can earn. Fulton himself linked the phrase both to the Cartesian logic of doubt and to the new green campus at Falmer, set a few miles from Brighton so that its members might think free from the distractions of the here-and-now.

Why it mattered

The pause as a defence

For Fulton and his contemporaries the cultivation of the pause was not a refinement but a necessity, and the reason was recent history. Sir Walter Moberly’s Crisis in the University (1949) had asked how the German universities, of the highest intellectual prestige in the world, could have failed to ‘repel doctrines morally monstrous and intellectually despicable’ under the Nazi regime. Part of the answer was a failure of the pause: a readiness to grant assent to ideology without the discipline of examining it first.

Fulton, who worked on the post-war reconstruction of the German universities, took the lesson to heart. A university that taught aproptosia, in epistemic, ethical, and political matters alike, would be harder to capture. This is why the discipline was built into the fabric of teaching: the weekly tutorial, in which a student’s thinking was examined under question, was the pause made into a habit (see Early History), and the schools of study were meant to resist the precipitate specialisation that produces expertise without judgement (see The Concept).

Be still and know… what?

The motto’s incompleteness is its point. By refusing to say what is to be known, Fulton left a question that each generation must answer for itself through the practice of the pause. In an age that rewards the instant reaction and the precipitate verdict, the discipline the motto names has become at once harder to sustain and more necessary than ever. It remains less a settled answer than a perpetual invitation: pause before judging; examine before assenting.
