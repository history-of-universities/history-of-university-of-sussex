A new university

A university designed, not inherited

When the University of Sussex received its Royal Charter in 1961, it was the first of a cohort of wholly new institutions that would come to be called the plate-glass universities: built at once, on green-field sites, in the modern materials of their decade. Sussex was the prototype. It opened with a deliberate ambition not merely to add a university but to rethink what a university curriculum could be. Its first Vice-Chancellor announced the Charter under the title Balliol by the Sea Faces Its Future, and the nickname stuck.

The idea had a long period of gestation, reaching back to a proposal of 1911. This site gathers that history in one place: the founding and its Charter, the building of the Falmer campus, the people who shaped it, the heraldry granted to it, and the long arc of the institution from the optimism of the 1960s through expansion, contraction, and reinvention to the present.

Why "plate glass"

The phrase comes from Michael Beloff's The Plate Glass Universities (1968), naming the new foundations of the 1960s by their architecture: not the stone of the ancient universities, nor the red brick of the Victorian civic ones, but plate glass and concrete. Sussex, chartered first, became the emblem of the type.
